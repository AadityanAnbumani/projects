## Getting Started 

### 1. The Brain: Arduino 🧠

For this project, I used an **Arduino Uno R4 Minima**.

<ImageZoom src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/codedex-io/projects/refs/heads/main/projects/build-a-pocket-rave-with-arduino/assets/uno.jpeg" />

If you are completely new to electronics, you might be wondering: *What on earth is an Arduino?*

Think of it as a tiny, pocket-sized computer. Just like your big laptop has a brain to think, memory to store things, and ports to plug in a mouse or keyboard, a microcontroller has all of those exact same things – just shrunk down to the size of a cracker!

However, it doesn’t have a built-in screen or a keyboard. It is completely blind and deaf until we plug things into it, and it doesn't know how to do anything until we give it a list of rules (our code). Once we type up our instructions and send them to the Arduino, it becomes the boss and tells all the connected lights, screens, and motors exactly what to do.

**Where to buy it:** You can grab the exact Arduino UNO R4 Minima I used from: 

- [Official Arduino Store](https://store-usa.arduino.cc/products/uno-r4-minima)
- [Amazon](https://a.co/d/0b24YgYf)

**Pro Tip: Swapping the Brain!** You don't have to use the Uno R4! Microcontrollers are like different breeds of dogs – they might look a little different, but they all love to play fetch. You can totally use an Arduino Nano, an ESP32 (which even has built-in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth!), or a Raspberry Pi Pico. As long as your new board has an I2C connection (more on that below!), you can throw the exact same pocket rave.

### 2. The Tiny TV: OLED Display 📺

This Tiny TV is a super small screen where our rave will actually happen. Even though it's tiny, it's bright enough to display crisp animations, text, and graphics.

**OLED** stands for **O**rganic **L**ight-**E**mitting **D**iode. That's just a fancy name that means "a screen made of thousands of teeny-tiny lightbulbs that can turn on/off individually." This display technology is found in modern TVs, monitors, laptops, smartphones, etc.

Our display has a technical name: the **SSD1306 128x64 OLED (I2C)**. 😵‍💫

<ImageZoom src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/codedex-io/projects/refs/heads/main/projects/build-a-pocket-rave-with-arduino/assets/oledwsong.jpeg" />

Let's break down that long name:

- SSD1306 is the display's model number.
- 128x64 means the screen is made up of a grid of tiny lights. 128 across and 64 down. Total of 8,192.
- I2C (Inter-Integrated Circuit) is the communication method the Arduino uses to talk to the display.

Think of I2C as a tiny, high-speed delivery tube. The Arduino drops instructions into the tube ("Turn on lightbulb at grid coordinate (10, 25)"), and the screen instantly follows the order almost instantly. That's why we only need those two special wires: 

- SDA (the data tube) 
- SCL (the clock that keeps the deliveries on schedule)

The good news? You don't need to understand this works! We'll use a library that handles all of it.

**Where to buy it:** [Amazon](https://a.co/d/00ZNDdmh).


### 3. The Power Line: USB-C Cable ⚡

To send our code from our laptop to the Arduino (and to give the Arduino power), we need a USB cable usually provided with the Arduino. The Arduino Uno R4 Minima uses a standard USB-C connection, so a regular phone charging/data cable works perfectly!


### 4. The Messengers: Jumper Wires 🪢

Finally, we need 4 Female-to-Male jumper wires. These are essentially strings of metal encased in colored plastic. They carry electricity and secret messages back and forth between the Brain and the Tiny TV.

**Where to buy it:** [Amazon](https://a.co/d/042vadtF).

## Software Setup

Before we plug our physical wires in, we need a place to write our instructions for the Brain. 

1. Download and install the free [Arduino IDE](https://www.arduino.cc/en/software) (Integrated Development Environment). Think of it as a fancy code editor, like VS Code or Builds, but specifically designed for writing hardware code in C++.
2. Open the IDE, look at the left sidebar, and click on the **Library Manager** (it looks like a stack of books).
3. We don't want to teach the Arduino how to draw a circle from scratch – that involves way too much math! Instead, search for and install two libraries: 
    - **Adafruit GFX Library**
    - **Adafruit SSD1306**

These libraries contain pre-written code that makes talking to our OLED screen super easy.

## Connecting the Dots (The Wiring)

How do we get the Brain to talk to the TV? We connect them with our 4 messenger wires! 

Here is the super easy secret to remembering how to wire this up forever:

<video width="100%" autoPlay loop muted playsInline>
  <source src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/codedex-io/projects/refs/heads/main/projects/build-a-pocket-rave-with-arduino/assets/wkConnection (1).mp4" type="video/mp4" />
</video>

- **VCC goes to 3.3V:** VCC is the "food." The TV needs energy to light up, so we plug it into the 3.3-volt power pin on the Arduino.
- **GND goes to GND:** This is the "Ground." It gives the electricity a safe place to go after it powers the TV. Think of it like a pair of shoes keeping everything grounded.
- **SDA goes to A4 (The Data Wire):** SDA carries the actual pictures and words to the screen.
- **SCL goes to A5 (The Clock Wire):** SCL keeps the rhythm and timing so the screen doesn't get confused.

***Just count the letters: SDA handles your DATA (4 letters = Pin A4), while SCL ticks the CLOCK ( 5 letters = Pin A5)***

## Writing the Magic Spells (The Code)

Now for the fun part: teaching the Brain how to throw a party using C++!

### 1. Giving the Brain a Dictionary

At the very top of our code, we write things like:

```cpp
#include <Wire.h>
#include <Adafruit_GFX.h>
#include <Adafruit_SSD1306.h>

#define SCREEN_WIDTH 128
#define SCREEN_HEIGHT 64
```

Think of these like giving the Arduino a dictionary. Instead of teaching the Arduino what a "screen" is from scratch, we just hand it a book that says, "Here is how you talk to the tiny TV."

The two `#define` lines is giving constant values some simple nicknames. Instead of typing the number 128 a bunch of times, we just tell the computer: "Whenever I say `SCREEN_WIDTH`, I mean 128."

These definitions specify the dimensions of a display (often an OLED screen):

- `SCREEN_WIDTH`: the display is 128 pixels wide
- `SCREEN_HEIGHT`: the display is 64 pixels tall

```output
          128 pixels
+----------------------------+
|                            |
|                            |  64 pixels
|                            |
+----------------------------+
```

### 2. Drawing with 1s and 0s

If you squint your eyes and look at the 1s in the array below, you can maybe see the shape of a heart! ♡

We save this "picture" under the name `heart_bmp[]` so we can stamp it on the screen later:

```cpp
const unsigned char heart_bmp[] PROGMEM = {
  0b01010000,
  0b11111000, 
  0b11111000,
  0b01110000,
  0b00100000
};
```

Remember, computers only understand two numbers: 1 (on) and 0 (off). 

To draw the little floating hearts on our screen, we literally draw a picture using 1s and 0s: 

- `1` tells the screen to turn a tiny lightbulb on.
- `0` tells the screen to leave it dark.

### 3. The Ultimate Secret: millis() vs delay()

Usually, when beginners want a computer to wait before doing the next thing, they use a command called `delay()`. But `delay()` is like telling the Arduino to go to sleep! If the Arduino is asleep, it can't update the lyrics and make the stick figure dance at the same time. Everything freezes.

To fix this, we use `millis()`. Instead of going to sleep, `millis()` tells the Arduino to constantly check a stopwatch. It says, "Keep looking at your watch! When 1.8 seconds pass, change the lyrics. When 230 milliseconds pass, make the guy dance!" This is called non-blocking code, and it lets our stick figure dance smoothly while the song lyrics scroll by!

### 4. The Floating Particle Effects (The Hearts)

To make the background look like a real party, we stamp our `heart_bmp` onto the screen. But we want them to float upwards!
  
On our screen, a Y-coordinate of 64 is the very bottom, and 0 is the very top.

```cpp
int heart1_Y = 64 - ((timeNow / 40) % 70);

display.drawBitmap(120, heart1_Y, heart_bmp, 5, 5, SSD1306_WHITE);
```

### 5. Choreographing the Dance

When people see the little guy dancing on the screen, they usually think it's a tiny video or a GIF. Nope! We are literally drawing him from scratch, 60 times a second, using a digital Etch-A-Sketch. 🕺

Snippets on how you can construct your own cutee Stick Man, Woman or even tun tun tun tun tun sahur ;-)

```cpp
display.drawCircle();
display.drawLine();
```

![jumping man with hearts gif](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/codedex-io/projects/refs/heads/main/projects/build-a-pocket-rave-with-arduino/assets/jumping-man.gif)

Instead of an image, we use math to draw shapes. How does he move? Remember our `millis()` stopwatch? Every 230 milliseconds, a variable called `danceBeat` flips between 0 and 1.

```cpp
int danceBeat = (timeNow / 230) % 2;
int guyY = 25 + (danceBeat == 1 ? -4 : 0);
```                                                                                                       

I love choreographing dances, so I had to make my stick figure bounce to the exact rhythm of the song. The song is 130 Beats Per Minute (BPM). Doing a little bit of math, that means every 230 milliseconds, a fast, club-style eighth-note beat hits!

Every time the stopwatch hits that 230ms mark, we tell the stick figure to throw his arms up and bounce his Y-coordinate up by 4 pixels.

### 6. The Digital Teleprompter

Now for the final piece... _drumroll, please_ 🥁... the lyrics! This is where you get to customize the code so it matches your favorite song.

We use an `if` / `else if` statement like a movie script. The Arduino checks our `millis()` stopwatch to see what "phase" of the song we are in, and prints the correct lyrics to the screen!

```cpp
if (phase == 0) {
  display.setCursor(0, 8);
  display.println("If you"); 
  display.println("go hard"); 
  display.println("you gotta");
} else if (phase == 1) {
  display.setCursor(0, 8);
  display.println("get on"); 
  display.println("the"); 
  display.println("floor");
}
```
